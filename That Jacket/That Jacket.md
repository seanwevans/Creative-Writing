<span class="underline">Introduction</span>

That night was long. Lying in a tepid puddle of my own sweat and
checking the time every five minutes was making me crazy. Somehow I’m
cold but still dripping with yellowing perspiration; it’s running off me
and pooling into the seams of the bare rubber mattress. Anxiety taps at
my sanity like a dog that doesn’t realize it’s ripping a door apart just
trying to get to the other side. Repeated, small dings at my
consciousness remind me constantly that sleep won’t come. I pray for
death.

A pair of headlights streams rudely through the open window as the
summer air delivers an aroma of oil-heavy car exhaust into the still
room. The echo of the old auto cuts off as the crickets reclaim their
summer night symphony. After some difficulty, a stranger’s silhouette
appears from the car, his face highlighted by a circular glow of light.
A fat, unkempt, man in an over-sized yellow trench coat slurps on a
cigar as he starts shambling his way towards the stone walkway that
leads to my sanctuary. There is no doubt, from any of my senses, that
the man is disgusting; I can smell him from my resting place. His coat
though, his coat is… amazing\! Almost supernaturally shiny, the coat
seems to shift and morph all over his body in an indescribable way. Just
looking at it hurts a little as the solid trench coat flows like a thick
liquid around this repulsive man. It subtly shifts over his body as it
changes shape while, at the same time, remaining all trenchcoat.

He doesn’t really walk, he sort of shuffles in a stiff leg waddle,
grunting a little with each punctuated step. To walk, it seems that he
jumps from leg to leg as if his knees just learned the secret the rest
of his body learned years ago, there’s no second breakfast in San
Francisco. As he approaches my door, and I’m awake this whole time, not
exactly watching but not not watching either, I can taste this stink on
the air. It’s unfamiliar and tangy, hard and musty.

It always seems longer than it actually is: that wait. That time between
when your brain says “Oh shit\! What the fuck is that?” and when it
realizes “Oh, it’s just that thing” can stretch out into eternity if you
let it. You can live in that moment as long as you want if you hit it
just right. If you focus on the correct things and feel the correct
emotions in the correct order at just the right time you can probably
stay in that blissful ignorance for as long as you want. After the story
starts but before the monsters come in is probably the best time to be
alive.

<span class="underline">Chapter 1</span>

Doris stood by the endless pier while she watched the gulls scream
across the coastline. The fine ocean mist tickled her face and she
wondered idly, when Charles would return. He had been gone three weeks
by this point and if this relationship was going to work then he would
damn well have to be a part of it\! She told herself that this would be
the last weekend she would wait and if he wanted to get serious before
then, well that would be fine with her. Besides, she wasn’t getting any
younger and when was the last time Charles took her out anyway? I mean
really took her out? Having convinced herself correct, Doris again
focused on the seagulls.

She stayed like that for a time, standing and waiting, wishing and
watching the seagulls, hoping that she had the courage to do what needed
to be done. To do what she came here to do. She steeled her breath and
turned on her heel as she briskly walked back down the pier towards the
angry place. Outside her destination a large three dimensional in your
face clown facade greeted her for what felt like the thousandth time.
Laughing in her face, the giant clown mouth gobbled up the storefront
while the happy dead eyes stared out across the ocean. The boardwalk
music rung distant in the background as Doris drowned out everything
around her. She had to focus. A shrill ringing began low in the back of
her head but she ignored it and continued forward through the clown’s
mouth and into the store.

This was it. This was the angry place. The place that couldn’t be
allowed anymore. Unacceptable\! Indecent\! It can’t be allowed to
continue. It will not\! I’ll stop it. Doris regained her composure as
she reached into her purse. Brushing against the cold steel of the .38
was reassuring in a way the seagulls could never be and she was glad she
decided to bring it along. This fucking clown won’t be laughing much
longer that’s for sure.

<span class="underline">Chapter 2</span>

The storefront was a maw. The painted wood and cheap plastic made
walking into the store more like being swallowed whole. The shrill
ringing in Doris’ head had upgraded to a steady throbbing. The raw
pressure behind her eyes, the noise of the machine and the smell of
6-year-old stale popcorn all assaulted her good conscience. The forced
joy of the carnival coupled with the sickly sweet spring of artificial
cotton candy aroma grated like sandpaper on bone.

The penultimate angry place: The location where happiness comes to die,
that’s where she was. Here, innocence sours and curdles. The fat,
sweat-stained man, wearing a paper hat and a sloppy asymmetric grin
leaned behind the counter as he picked idly at a sore on his arm. He
watched from the inside as she allowed herself to be swallowed whole by
the entrance and farted as she approached.

The hot steel of her revolver was the only thing keeping her physically
anchored now. Knuckles white on her purse strap. *Unacceptable.
Indecent. Disallowed.* Thoughts hammered in her head like a waterfall of
bad times, the mechanical *ding* in the background of the games seeming
to punctuate her grief at regular intervals. She allowed herself a
moment to live in the aftermath, to experience the dead silence. Aside
from the gulls and the ocean spray, it sounded like bliss.

Stopping in front of the counter, directly in front of the man, the
smell hit her all at once, which woke her up a little. As he looks up
from his scab she opens her purse and fondles the gun. By the time she
grips it and extends her arm, the slow deliberate actions of a person
full of resolve, the moment is almost here. She lives here between the
shot and the consequence, but it’s always too fleeting. As her finger
makes the pilgrimage from resting position to fired and the time runs
out of the room, the echo of the blast acts as her alarm. It’s done,
she’s awake again: The back of that disgusting cigar-slurper’s head
was, all of a sudden, all over the back wall in many discrete pieces.

And she walked. The clown vomited.
