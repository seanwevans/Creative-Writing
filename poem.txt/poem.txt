The workers arrive before the sun—

Brown coats, metal ID tags.

Why does everything always smell like soap?

It’s always `units processed`.

They travel in groups; stragglers get singled out.

Someone hums a song no one recognizes.

A child’s glove lies in the mud outside the gate.

The piston falls,

the steam hisses,

the counter clicks upward.

“Efficiency up one-point-six percent this quarter!”

The line supervisor nods, pleased, as he jots the number down.

The siren barks the end of shift.

A man hoses crimson dirt toward the floor drain.

The steam smells metallic.

A clipboard slaps shut; laughter echoes down a corridor.

The factory has met its quota.

There is nothing to worry about—jobs are safe.

Outside, trains idle, engines like mechanical hearts.

Boxcars breathe mist through slats in a slow, persistent rhythm.

The men light cigarettes, watching the dawn bleed across unyielding
smokestacks.

Somewhere beyond, a lowing rises.

Today's beef has arrived.
