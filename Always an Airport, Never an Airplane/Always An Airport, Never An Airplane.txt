“You know what my problem is?” Elias Vance said as he pitched a card
across the table to Adrian Cross. “I never get to be specific.” Adrian
peeled one corner of his hand and frowned at it with professionally
managed disappointment.

“That is not your problem.”

“It is absolutely my problem.”

“No,” Adrian said, constructing a skyscraper of red chips, “Your problem
is that you still think you’re a lead.”

Ethan Vale laughed too hard as he leaned back and rubbed his face
“Jesus.”

Across from him, Nolan Hayes had a stillness some mistook for modesty.
In his case it was mostly fatigue.

“He’s right, though,” Nolan said. “Specificity is expensive. You only
get that if somebody important loves you.”

Marcus Reed glanced at his cards, folded immediately, and sat back with
the posture of a man waiting to be debriefed. “Or if the writer had a
father he’s trying to forgive.”

Caleb Stone grinned. “That’s good.”

“It’s true!” Marcus said. “Nobody writes us from life. They write us
from the leftovers. We’re the turkey sandwich after Thanksgiving.”

Elias sighed and tossed in two chips. “There. That. That’s what I mean.
I never get that texture. I get the broad-strokes only. ‘Capable but
haunted.’ Haunted by what? Name one thing. Give me a county. Give me a
vocational certificate. Give me an ex-wife with a weird hobby. I would
kill for a weird hobby.”

“You don’t want a hobby,” Adrian said. “A hobby is death.”

“How is a hobby death?”

“They give hobbies to men they don’t intend to develop. Woodworking, fly
tying, restoring old watches. It’s set dressing for the illusion of
interiority.”

Caleb pointed at him. “Interiority. Listen to this bastard.”

Adrian ignored him. “A real lead gets contradiction. We don’t get shit.”

Ethan tossed in chips with unnecessary flourish. “I had contradiction
once.”

“No you didn’t,” said Nolan.

“I absolutely did.”

“You had a voice-over.”

“That is a kind of contradiction.”

“No,” Adrian said. “it’s not.”

Ethan stared at him. “You really are unpleasant.”

Adrian just grunted.

The flop came down. Nobody cared.

Elias looked at the cards like they might offer grounds for appeal. “You
know what I’m tired of? Airports.”

A murmur of agreement moved around the table.

“Always the airport,” said Marcus.

“Always walking with purpose,” Nolan added.

“Always no destination,” said Caleb. “Just concourse B to fuck-all.”

Ethan sat forward, pointing with two fingers like he was trying to land
an important monologue. “Exactly. Exactly. They give you your own story
for ten beautiful minutes and where does it happen? Airport terminal.
Rental car shuttle. Anonymous hotel bar. Places designed to imply
transition because nobody intends for you to arrive.”

“There’s a sex joke in there somewhere,” Elias said quietly.

“Thank you.”

Adrian rolled his eyes. “It’s funny because it’s true. They keep us in
non-places. Transitional architecture for transitional characters.”

Nolan drummed two fingers against his chip stack. “It’s containment.”

Everyone looked at him.

He shrugged. “You put a generic man in a specific place, people start
asking questions. Why’s he here? What does he know? Why does he move
like that? But put him in an airport, a garage, a motel corridor, an
overpass in the rain, and nobody asks for dick. The setting does the
work. He becomes plausible by proximity.”

Marcus nodded once. “You’re saying location launders character?”

“That’s exactly what it is,” Adrian said. “We are carriers for ‘the
mood’.”

No one spoke for a moment after that. A few chips changed hands.
Somewhere in the house, ice clinked in a glass. The ceiling fan
continued its oblong orbit. A dog sneezed.

Then Caleb, who had been smiling with the patient warmth of a man about
to ruin someone’s evening, said, “I got killed in a grain elevator
once.”

Everyone turned.

“You what?”

“Grain elevator,” Caleb repeated. “Nebraska, maybe. Could’ve been
Kansas. Script didn’t care. I had my own thing going for nearly twenty
minutes. Estranged brother, local rumors, little bit of charm, hint of
alcoholism but not the ugly kind. Just enough to make me
‘misunderstood’. Then, bang! Grain dust, muzzle flash, down I go. Never
even found out if I was dirty.”

Elias pointed at him accusingly. “Exactly. That’s exactly the kind of
role I wanted.”

“No, you don’t,” Caleb said. “That’s the worst of both worlds. Too much
attention to disappear, not enough to survive.”

Marcus lifted a finger. “I had pulp once.”

They all went quiet.

“Paperback pulp or prestige pretending to be pulp?” Adrian asked.

“Paperback paperback,” Marcus said. “Real stuff. Desert town. Missing
girls. Corrupt sheriff. I played a mechanic with a gambling problem and
a war record the script kept circling like it was scared to touch. For
six pages I thought, finally, this is it. This is where I become
somebody.”

“What happened?” Someone asked.

Marcus stared at the felt. “Fuckin chapter nineteen happened! Turned out
the whole book was really about a drifter who may or may not have been
an angel.”

“Oof, that’s brutal,” Nolan said.

“Yeah, well.”

“You got angel-jacked.”

“I got fuckin’ angel-jacked.”

Ethan leaned back, shaking his head in sincere grief. “No recovering
from that.”

Adrian tapped the table twice for the dealer that wasn’t there. “At
least pulp lets you sweat. I’d take sweat. I’d take one frayed motel
towel and an actual honest-to-god vice.”

“You don’t have a vice?” Caleb asked.

“Eh, only ambiguities.”

“That’s not vice.”

“It is if it ruins your life.”

Elias finally looked down at his own hand again. “I got one episode once
where they hinted I painted.”

Nolan looked up. “Painted what?”

“Never said.”

Caleb squinted. “Seriously?”

“Dead serious. There was a line. Somebody asked why I had turpentine on
my sleeve. I said:

‘Old habit.’

That was it.”

“That’s incredible,” Ethan said reverently. “That is the cruelest thing
I’ve ever heard.”

“I know.”

“Turpentine on the sleeve is loads better than anything I’ve ever
gotten.”

Marcus considered. “It at least implies a room somewhere.”

“Yes,” Elias said, with the bitterness of a man discussing a dead child.
“A room. Maybe with light. Maybe with unfinished canvases. Maybe with
one painting turned to the wall. We’ll never know.”

Adrian pinched the bridge of his nose. “This is how they keep us docile
you know? Fragments.”

Nolan nodded. “Just enough off-screen life that the audience does the
labor for free.”

Caleb tossed in a call and smiled at nobody. “Boys ever notice we only
get names like ours when they want to suggest seriousness without
risking anything else?”

“That’s the whole game,” Adrian said. “Elias Vance. Adrian Cross. Nolan
Hayes. Ethan Vale. Marcus Reed. Caleb Stone. We sound like six options
in a dropdown menu labeled ‘male, 35 to 48.’”

“Forty-nine,” Marcus muttered.

“No chance,” said Adrian. “They will never let you be forty-nine.
Forty-nine has history. You’re forty-six in a hard way or thirty-eight
in a tired way.”

Ethan pointed across the table. “That’s true. They age us by adjective,
not number.”

Nolan laughed at that, softly. “That’s true.”

For the first time all night, they laughed together for a second, but it
floated like a lump over the table.

Then Elias said, “You know what I really want?”

Silence.

“I want to be in a story where I am allowed to be bad at something.”

He went on. “Not broken. Not haunted. Not secretly brilliant under
pressure. Just bad. I want to fumble with a radiator. I want to lose at
chess to a child. I want to burn dinner and pretend it was intentional.
I want one scene where somebody says, ‘Elias, you are a strange little
man,’ but they mean it with love.”

Caleb looked at him with surprising tenderness. “That’s strong.”

Marcus nodded and agreed. “That’s real strong.”

Adrian’s face shifted almost imperceptibly, which for him was
practically weeping. “Competence can be a prison.”

Ethan gestured broadly with both hands. “Yes.” he continued. “We’re all
competence and weather damage. No wonder nobody finishes our stories.
There’s nowhere to go. We enter the frame 82 percent complete.”

Nolan took a sip from his drink. “That’s why they abandon us in
airports.”

Outside, a car passed through the wet streetlight glow beyond the
curtained window. Nobody looked. It was not that kind of story.

Finally, Caleb said, “I had a daughter once.”

Five heads snapped toward him.

“You did not,” Adrian said.

“I did for thirteen pages.”

Ethan was already laughing. “No.”

“Swear to God. She drew horses. There was a box in the garage. I found
it while looking for a lug wrench or some such crap. Real nice scene.
Quiet. Human. Thought I was about to become a person.”

Marcus leaned in. “What happened to her?”

“They cut her in rewrites.”

A silence fell so total it nearly counted as prayer.

“That,” said Nolan, “is the saddest sentence ever spoken.”

Caleb nodded. “Producer said she made me too sympathetic.”

Adrian sat back in disgust. “Of course. Can’t have that. Then the
audience might start tracking your emotional arc.”

Elias looked sick. “A daughter who drew horses.”

“Yep.”

“Jesus.”

“I know.”

Ethan ran both hands through his hair. “This whole business is evil.”

“Wrose,” Adrian said. “It’s lazy.”

The turn card landed. Marcus, who had folded twenty minutes ago, stared
at it as if it had personally failed him.

“What would your stories actually be?” Nolan asked.

That changed the air.

Not because it was a grand question, but because it was too sincere. Men
like them were used to speaking in diagnosis, not desire.

Of course Ethan recovered first.

“Mine?” He sat up. “Small coastal town. Off-season. I run a failing
movie theater inherited from an uncle I never respected enough when he
was alive. I tell everyone I’m staying only until probate clears, but I
start restoring the place. There’s mold, debt, a broken marquee, and I
slowly become a pillar of a community I was too self-important to value
in my thirties.”

Caleb pointed at him. “That’s pretty good.”

Marcus said, “Who’s the woman?”

“There doesn’t have to be a woman.”

“There’s a woman.”

Ethan hesitated. “Local journalist.”

Adrian smirked. “Of course.”

“She’s tired,” Ethan continued. “Sharper than me. Not impressed.
Divorced, maybe.”

“Of course,” Adrian said again.

“But,” Ethan said, lifting a finger, “the important thing is the theater
matters as much as the romance.”

Nolan nodded. “That’s not bad.”

Elias said, “I’d watch that.”

Adrian exhaled through his nose. “Mine is not for mass consumption.”

“God help us,” Caleb said.

Adrian ignored him. “Mine is about municipal corruption.”

Everyone groaned.

“No, listen. Small city. Procurement office. I am not a cop, not a
prosecutor, not a former operative. I’m a deputy comptroller. I discover
a pattern in waste management contracts and realize half the city’s
patronage machine runs through one family-owned hauling company. No
guns. No car chases. Just paper, pressure, and the gradual realization
that every decent person I know has made peace with filth.”

There was a pause.

Marcus slowly nodded. “Yeah, that’s…, wow.”

Nolan agreed.

Caleb squinted. “Would there still be a scene in a parking garage?”

“Of course.”

“Then you’re still sick.”

Elias looked down at his cards, then set them face down. “Mine’s about
painting.”

“Forty-two. Divorced a long time ago. Lives above a hardware store in a
river town no one has heard of. Teaches art to three retirees and one
difficult twelve-year-old. Was almost something once, maybe, but it
doesn’t matter. Story starts after the disappointment. That’s the point.
Nothing is circling some old glory. It’s just about whether he can make
one honest thing before he dies.”

Nobody made fun of him.

Even Adrian did not make fun of him.

Marcus broke the silence. “That’s the best one so far.”

Ethan nodded. “Yeah.”

Caleb said, “The twelve-year-old steals every scene.”

“He has to,” Elias replied.

Nolan glanced at each of them in turn, then said, “Mine’s simpler. I
want a hardware store.”

They blinked. “A hardware store?”

“Yeah.” He shrugged. “Open at six. Coffee always terrible. Regulars with
ongoing nonsense. I know where everything is. Storm coming, everybody
needs weird little parts they can’t identify. We solve it together.
Maybe my brother owes taxes. Maybe the roof needs fixing. Maybe I
sponsor a girls softball team and take it too seriously. That’s enough.”

Marcus leaned back and smiled for the first time. “That’s more than
enough.”

Caleb lifted his glass. “To the hardware store.”

They all lifted something to that.

Marcus looked down at the table. “How about a mechanic?”

“You were a mechanic,” Ethan said.

“No. I was mechanic-shaped. That’s different. I want the real one. Shop
outside Tucson, maybe. Or Ohio. Somewhere unglamorous on purpose. Men
bring me sounds they can’t understand in old engines and I know what
they mean only because I’ve listened a long time.”

No one spoke for a moment.

Then Adrian said, very quietly, “That’s good.”

Marcus nodded once. “Thanks.”

All eyes turned to Caleb Stone.

He smiled his infuriatingly trustworthy smile and spread his hands. “I
already had mine.”

“The daughter with the horses?” Elias asked.

“No.” Caleb leaned back. “Mine’s a campground.”

They waited.

“I own it. Nothing fancy. Cabins, fishing pond, busted vending machine.
I’m a widower, but not fresh. We’re not milking it. It happened years
ago. I’m raising a nephew who lies a lot but in a creative way. Every
week some strange little problem comes through. Lost dog, flooded trail,
church retreat feud, raccoon in the laundry room, teenager discovers
poetry, old man cheats at cribbage. That sort of thing.”

Ethan stared. “That’s insanely good.”

“I know,” Caleb said.

“The nephew is great,” Marcus said.

“The nephew is incredible,” Nolan agreed.

Adrian looked almost offended and Caleb’s grin widened.

They played a few hands in thoughtful silence after that, then Ethan,
unable to leave sincerity unpunctured for too long, said, “You know
they’d still put one of us in an airport.”
