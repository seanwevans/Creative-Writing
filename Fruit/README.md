In the long night of hunger, Adam and Eve wandered, exiled, beneath the
gaze of the ancient tree. The gnarled bark smacked of smoky earth and
musty swamp. Eve knelt among roots veined with glowing fungus, knowing
Adam wouldn’t last much longer without food. She freed the fungus from
the ground, broke the strange fruit into small pieces, and placed them
into Adam’s trembling hands.

“This is the flesh of the earth”, she said in their dialect as they both
ate heavily from nature’s bountiful bouquet.

The small glowing mushrooms were scattered everywhere around the elder
tree, their light poking through the tall grasses in sharp spikes of
dusted brilliance.

They ate so many mushrooms. And then the shared visions came:

A branching, infinite tree in the sky, where every leaf was a possible
world, every root a memory given as a gift by a loving cosmos. A cosmic
snake appeared to each and whispered epic universal secrets that were
known by none.

“Count”

“Choose”

The secrets kept coming. And that was only the first night.

By that sacramental meal, the couple was remade: No longer were they
hungry for food; they had all they could eat\! But as one hunger is
sated another arises: The hunger of understanding.

They stayed under the tree, eating the magical fungus, tripping and
loving each other fiercely--birthing civilization.

They pioneered critical thinking, proto-mathematics and long-term
planning. They taught all of this to their children when they came of
age (an idea whispered by the fungus).

From their camp beneath the Tree of Life, a new tribe arose--a peculiar
people, with the strangest of customs. Around the fire, they marked the
ground with numbers and patterns, planning their hunts to perfection,
lost fewer to injury, and began a kind of methodical scientific
experimentation.

Each season, as the mushrooms sprouted after a sacred rain, the tribe
would gather, led by a medicine man. They came together for a grand
fungus feast beneath the tree, sharing a common vision. Each member of
the tribe sought their own form of guidance from the mother tree, but
the experience was shared as a whole society.

Ritual had been born. Eating to survive, eating for tradition, eating to
dream and create, eating to understand. The act became myth, which
mutated into law, eventually becoming legend and memory.

Many thousands of years later, a great forgetting swept the world-a
flood of water and time. Cities drowned, tribes scattered, stories were
lost. In the aftermath, survivors built atop the memories of ruins.

Holy men reasoned that a new covenant with the earth was required. No
longer could the knowledge of the sacred meal be shared freely. It was
now a secret that must be hidden, lest some fool eat too many sacred
mushrooms.

As generations passed, the world, of course, forgot and grew new tribes
and expanded her hold through humanity. Each group had their own gods,
their own rites, and some discovered other sacred fruits of the earth.
Some remembered the tree unconsciously, but most had forgotten its
roots.

Alternate systems of worship began to spin-off, like a bad game of
telephone, echoing throughout the ages. Those who controlled the flow of
knowledge began to piece together the past and its relevant details, but
by that time, they had decided the knowledge was too dangerous for the
public to consume.

Mushrooms became bread.

The feast became transubstantiation.

The mother tree of life became the cross.

Other smatterings of details across scripture were corrupted as further
safeguards, to keep the uninitiated from finding purchase. This, of
course, would have the added benefit of cementing the current power
structure, ensuring the continued rule of those in power over those not.
