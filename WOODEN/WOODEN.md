*WOODEN*

*Sean Evans*

***Chapter 1 — A4** "Still Warm" The opening paragraph. Jerry closes the
laptop. The heart on the white napkin. The blood fresh on his son's
lips. We are deep in the delusion — it has furniture, it has routine, it
has the warm literary voice of a man who has completely solved the
problem of his grief. The son moves through the house wrongly. Jerry
notices everything and understands nothing. His professional eye for
fluid animation is fully operational and entirely misdirected. The
recording equipment is visible but unremarked. One deniable wrongness
planted quietly — the son does something with his body that no living
thing does — and Jerry's journal moves past it in half a sentence.*

Jerry Pereto closed the lid on his laptop as he clicked off the light on
his desk. The child's heart that he had placed on a white linen napkin
on his work bench was still warm and the smell of clean iron, blood, was
still fresh on his son's lips.

***Chapter 2 — B5** "Case Notes" The detective. Mid-investigation. She
already has the sister's testimony — the sister who stopped returning
calls, who Jerry's journal mentions once as being bad at sitting with
difficult emotions. She has neighbor accounts. She has a missing child
from three streets over whose absence has a shape she's beginning to
recognize. She doesn't have the house yet. She doesn't have Jerry's name
yet. But she has the pattern and the pattern has an address at its
center she's one warrant away from. The reader realizes with mounting
dread that the warmth of chapter 1 has a body count already attached to
it.*

***Chapter 3 — A3** "Reference Footage" Earlier. The rituals are newer
here, less settled into habit. The son is more overtly wrong — the dumb
demon hasn't learned the controls as well yet, bumps into the furniture
of human behavior more visibly. Jerry's explanations are longer now,
working harder, a paragraph where a sentence used to do. We see the
recording begin — Jerry frames it to himself as documentation of
recovery, clinical, professional, the way you'd track an animation
problem across builds. His fluid animation vocabulary bleeds into his
description of the son crossing a room. The laptop is open more often
now. He is studying something.*

***Chapter 4 — B6** "The Boy Who Went To The Library" The detective
finds the friend — or finds where the friend was. Seventeen years old,
the one who knew the son best, the one who went looking for an
explanation in local history and folklore and found something that
scared him badly enough to tell an adult. That adult is now also a data
point in the detective's case file. The house is under loose
surveillance. A neighbor reports a smell. The pattern the detective has
been building suddenly has a logic to it she almost wishes she hadn't
found — because the logic implies a mind, and the mind implies the thing
she's been circling is not random.*

***Chapter 5 — A2** "Getting Better" Earlier still. The son has not been
back long. The justifications are forming in real time — the reader
watches the architecture of the delusion being constructed from raw
grief and desperate love. Something happens in this chapter that should
end the delusion entirely. A moment of pure unmistakable wrongness that
any outside observer would find indefensible. Jerry builds an
explanation so internally coherent and so completely insane that the
reader recognizes this as the novel's most dangerous chapter. His
intelligence fully deployed in the wrong direction. The recording is
framed here as something almost tender — I want to remember every stage
of his recovery.*

***Chapter 6 — B7** "Exhibit A" They get the laptop. The detective
watches the footage in an evidence room and the novel's second
structural detonation happens here. The footage and the journal are
documents so incompatible they seem to describe different universes —
except the handwriting matches and the voice matches and the man in the
background of several frames matches the name on the warrant. The B
chapters go almost clinical here. Forensic. Timestamp by timestamp. The
detective is reconstructing not just a crime but a mind, and the mind is
more disturbing than the crime because it is so recognizably human and
so completely elsewhere.*

***Chapter 7 — A1** "The Date" The earliest A chapter. Jerry closest to
the accident. The son barely back, barely settled, still carrying
whatever he brought with him like luggage he hasn't unpacked. Jerry is
at his most raw here — the prose simpler, the explanations barely
formed, a man still in the process of deciding to believe rather than a
man who has already built a cathedral around that belief. His grief is
visible here in a way it isn't in the later chapters. The reader who has
watched him become the chapter 4 man now watches him become that man in
real time, and it is heartbreaking and terrible in equal measure. The
entry ends mid-sentence. He started to write something true and
stopped.*

***Chapter 8 — B8** "Wooden" The raid. Everything from outside. The
detective's POV on the house, the door coming open, what they find, what
the son has finally done with the controls now that he's found them. His
destruction — not a death exactly, more like an ending, a switch being
thrown — and Jerry's response. The molotov. Handmade. A man who builds
things building one last thing from whatever is available. The arrest.
Then the evidence room, the folder, the journal in the detective's
hands. She reads the last entry. The unfinished sentence. She closes the
folder. She stands up. She walks down the hall.*

***Chapter 9 — A0** "The Ground" Neither A nor B. Jerry's mind during
the arrest, stripped of the delusion by catastrophe, in free fall
backward through memory toward the last moment he had something to hold.
A hillside at night. His son's weight. The Micmac ground receiving what
he brought it. The prose is flat, spare, almost biblical — the clearest
Jerry has been in the entire novel arriving simultaneously with the
worst thing he has ever done. His internal monologue in italics,
intruding into the flat prose: he just needs rest. this place will help
him. The chapter ends on Jerry opening a new journal entry. Writing the
date. Nothing after it. He was waiting to see what came back. Down the
hall a door opens. The reader already knows exactly what happens in the
room beyond it. The book closes because it has nothing left to
withhold.*
